Path

Direct to State

The Internetworking category at my school had no regional round — I went directly to the Florida State Competition. Qualified through SouthTech Academy and prepared independently over several weeks leading up to the event.

How the Competition Works

  • Written exam — covers Layer 2 and Layer 3 networking concepts: how switches forward frames using MAC addresses, how routers forward packets using IP addresses, subnetting, and network device roles.
  • Oral/sales scenario — presented with a buyer scenario (similar to an MSP client consultation) and required to explain and recommend the right networking equipment and solution for their needs.
  • No hands-on lab component at states for this category — the exam and scenario tested conceptual depth and the ability to communicate technical decisions clearly to a non-technical audience.

How I Prepared

  • Studied L2/L3 concepts through CCNA-level material — packet structure, MAC vs. IP forwarding, switching behavior, routing fundamentals.
  • Practiced hands-on with my SG300 switch — actually configuring and experimenting rather than just reading about it.
  • Over-prepared deliberately: showed up with a full box of gear — two laptops, crimpers, RJ45 strippers, console cables, DB9 adapters, a power strip. Turned out I didn't need any of it, but I wasn't going to be caught without something I needed.
  • Spent weeks stressing the details — right console cables, right adapters, right tools. That level of preparation is what got me ready to answer anything on the day.

What This Shows Employers

SkillsUSA Internetworking tests both technical knowledge and the ability to communicate it — two things that matter every day in networking and IT support roles. Winning at the state level against peers who also prepared seriously means the fundamentals are solid. The oral scenario specifically mirrors what MSP and help desk technicians do: understand a client's environment, identify what they need, and explain it clearly. That's not a classroom exercise — that's the job.
