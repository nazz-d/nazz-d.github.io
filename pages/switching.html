Cisco Switching & DMZ VLAN

Two Cisco Catalyst 3750 V2 switches configured with trunk ports, VLAN 50 DMZ isolation, and SSH-based administration — providing clean traffic separation between the native LAN and a public ingress path for Cloudflare Tunnel.

Cisco Catalyst 3750 V2 VLAN Segmentation Trunk Ports DMZ Design SSH Administration Change Validation

What I Built

  • Deployed two Cisco Catalyst 3750 V2 switches in a main/sub hierarchy — the main switch is the STP root for both VLAN 1 and VLAN 50, with the sub switch uplinked via a trunk port.
  • Designed VLAN 50 as a DMZ for public-facing services, keeping all internet-exposed workloads isolated from the native LAN (VLAN 1) at the switching layer.
  • Configured trunk ports between OPNsense, Proxmox, the main switch, and the sub switch — all carrying VLANs 1 and 50 with VLAN 1 as the native VLAN.
  • Cleaned up switch administration: created a named local account, removed the default admin account, and verified SSH access before removing the old credential.
  • Validated the full path after every change — DHCP lease delivery, ping to the DMZ gateway, tunnel service status, and trunk allowlist review on both switches.

Design

Main / Sub Switch Hierarchy

The main Catalyst 3750 V2 acts as the aggregation point — it uplinks directly to OPNsense and Proxmox, and trunks down to the sub switch. Placing the main switch as STP root for both VLANs ensures predictable forwarding and avoids accidental topology loops as the lab grows. The sub switch inherits the same VLAN allowlist through its uplink trunk.

Isolation

VLAN 50 DMZ for Public Ingress

VLAN 50 (Public_DMZ, 192.168.x.x/24) exists to hold the Cloudflare Tunnel container and any other internet-facing workloads. Traffic from the DMZ subnet cannot reach LAN hosts without going through OPNsense firewall rules — the VLAN boundary enforces separation at layer 2 before any routing decision is made. The native LAN stays clean regardless of what happens in the DMZ.

Validation

Testing Every Change

After trunking VLAN 50 through both switches, I confirmed the full path: the DMZ container received a DHCP lease from OPNsense, could ping the DMZ gateway, and the Cloudflare Tunnel service reported active registered connections. I also reviewed the trunk allowlist on both switches over SSH to confirm the config matched intent — not just that the service came up.

What This Shows Employers

Managed Cisco switching is day-one relevant for junior network admin, NOC, and MSP roles. I configured real Catalyst hardware — not a simulator — and made deliberate design choices: STP root placement, DMZ isolation at layer 2, explicit trunk allowlists, and credential hygiene on the switches themselves. The validation process mirrors what a production change requires: confirm DHCP, confirm routing, confirm the service, review the config. That's the workflow, not just the outcome.
